---
title: 巴菲特股东信读后：十点洞察（1977—2024）
date: 2026-07-20
---

本文并非逐年通读巴菲特48年间致股东的全部信件，而是深入抽取了几封关键节点的原始信件——1977年首封信、1989年"错误"总结信、1996年"股东手册"、2008年金融危机信、2014年"五十周年"回顾（含巴菲特本人与芒格各自独立撰写的两篇文章）、以及2024年信——从中提炼出十条有据可查的洞察，而非泛泛而谈的语录汇编。

一、**ROE（净资产收益率），而非每股收益增长，才是真正的记分牌。**（1977年信）巴菲特否定了"每股收益增长"作为衡量指标的意义——一旦净资产基数在扩大，这个数字就会失真；真正诚实的衡量标准是净资产收益率。他用这把尺子去衡量伯克希尔当年19%的净资产收益率，既对照自身历史，也对照全行业水平。

二、**错失的代价，远大于犯错的代价——而且两位合伙人各自独立得出了同样的结论。**在2014年的信中，巴菲特与芒格分别撰写了各自的文章，且都未读过对方的初稿。芒格写道："几乎所有巨大的失误，都是没有出手买进造成的，包括没有买入沃尔玛的股票……如果当年抓住了那几次机会，伯克希尔现在的净资产至少要高出500亿美元。"巴菲特在2008年的信里表达了同样的意思："我犯了一些不作为的错误——面对新出现的事实，我本该重新审视自己的判断并及时行动，却选择了迟疑。"

三、**巴菲特自己一生中最大的一笔错误交易，就是买下伯克希尔本身。**（2014年信）1965年接手伯克希尔，用他自己的话说，是"一个愚蠢至极的决定"——起因不过是西伯利·斯坦顿在握手谈好的价格上硬生生扣掉八分之一点，让他一时意气用事，而不是理性分析的结果。代价更大的一笔错误，是他把国民保险公司（National Indemnity）买进了伯克希尔（当时61%由外部股东持有），而不是买进他自己完全掌控的合伙企业BPL——他估算，这一决定把大约"1000亿美元"的财富，从他的合伙人手里转移给了一群与他毫无关系的伯克希尔旧股东。"这个问题我想了48年，至今也没想出一个像样的答案。"

四、**芒格的一句话，重新设计了整套打法。**"忘掉你懂得的那套'用便宜的价格买一般的公司'，转而'用合理的价格买一家真正卓越的公司'。"这句话促成了1972年对喜诗糖果（See's Candy）的收购——花费2500万美元买下，此后至今已累计贡献了19亿美元的税前利润，而追加投入的资本只有4000万美元。

五、**浮存金，是那台安静运转的复利引擎。**保险浮存金（持有但不属于自己的资金）从1970年的3900万美元，增长到2024年的1710亿美元——这主要得益于伯克希尔多年来大部分年份都能实现承保利润，也就是说，不但免费使用这笔钱，有时甚至因为持有它而获得额外报酬。

六、**看人重品行，而非学历——这一点被明确写了出来。**（2024年信）"我从不看候选人毕业于哪所学校。从不！"他举了皮特·利格尔（Pete Liegl）的例子——凭一次握手就把森林河公司（Forest River）卖给了伯克希尔，自己开口定薪10万美元；还有本·罗斯纳（Ben Rosner）——"从没上过六年级以上的学"。巴菲特的结论是："商业才能很大程度上是天生的，天赋压过了后天培养。"

七、**危机中的纪律，是提前准备好的，不是临场发挥的。**（2008年信）"投资时，悲观是你的朋友，狂热才是敌人。"2008年恐慌期间，伯克希尔在三周内向美国企业投放了156亿美元——之所以能做到，是因为公司始终保留至少200亿美元的国库券。巴菲特把现金比作"氧气——存在时无人在意，一旦缺失便成了唯一在意的事"。

八、**对"用公式取代判断"始终保持警惕。**（2008年信，针对布莱克-舒尔斯期权定价公式被错误套用到百年期期权上）"提防那些手持公式的书呆子。"（2014年信）"波动性远远不等同于风险……把两者划等号的流行公式，正在把学生、投资者和CEO们引入歧途。"

九、**对失败的坦诚，是可以量化的习惯。**（2024年信）巴菲特提到，仅2019至2023这五年间，他在信中使用"错误"或"失误"这两个词就多达16次，而许多同等规模的大公司"从未使用过这两个词中的任何一个"。他对接班人格雷格·阿贝尔的告诫是："一旦你开始欺骗股东，用不了多久你自己也会开始相信那些谎话，最终连自己也被自己蒙骗了。"

十、**企业集团的结构，本质上是一台"避税＋减少摩擦成本"的机器，而不只是简单的多元化。**（2014年信）伯克希尔可以"不产生税负、也几乎不产生其他成本"地在各项业务之间调拨资金——这是任何单一投资者或单一行业公司都不具备的优势，让喜诗糖果这样成熟的现金牛业务产生的利润，可以直接拿去买新业务，而不必像独立公司那样，先把利润分给股东、被课一道税，股东再自行决定如何再投资。

参考资料：原始信件——1977年、1989年、1996年、2008年、2014年、2024年（均来自伯克希尔哈撒韦官网 berkshirehathaway.com/letters/letters.html）。

This isn't a mechanical read-through of all 48 years of Buffett's letters, but a deep sampling of several pivotal ones — the original 1977 letter, the 1989 "mistakes" letter, the 1996 "Owner's Manual," the 2008 crisis letter, the full 2014 50th-anniversary retrospective (including separate essays by both Buffett and Munger, each written without reading the other's), and the 2024 letter — distilled into ten sourced insights rather than a generic quote compilation.

1. **ROE, not EPS growth, is the real scorecard.** (1977) Buffett rejected earnings-per-share growth as a meaningful metric once the equity base is expanding — the honest measure is return on equity capital, and he judged Berkshire's 19% ROE that year against both its own history and industry aggregates.

2. **Errors of omission dwarf errors of commission — and both partners said so, independently.** In the 2014 letter, Buffett and Munger each wrote their own essay without reading the other's first. Munger's line: "almost all huge errors were in not making a purchase, including not purchasing Walmart stock... Berkshire's net worth would now be at least $50 billion higher." Buffett makes the same point in 2008 about "errors of omission, sucking my thumb when new facts came in."

3. **Buffett's own worst mistake was buying Berkshire itself.** (2014) Taking control in 1965 was, in his words, "a monumentally stupid decision" — born of pique at Seabury Stanton shaving an eighth of a point off a handshake price, not analysis. His even costlier mistake: buying National Indemnity *inside* Berkshire (61%-owned by outsiders) rather than through his own partnership BPL, which he calculates diverted "$100 billion or so" from his partners to unrelated shareholders. "I've had 48 years to think about that question, and I've yet to come up with a good answer."

4. **One sentence from Munger rewired the whole strategy.** "Forget what you know about buying fair businesses at wonderful prices; instead, buy wonderful businesses at fair prices." This turned the 1972 See's Candy purchase ($25M) into the template — $1.9 billion pre-tax earned since, on just $40 million of added investment.

5. **Float is the quiet compounding engine.** Insurance float (money held but not owned) grew from $39M in 1970 to $171B in 2024, largely because Berkshire runs an underwriting profit in most years — meaning it gets paid to hold and invest other people's money.

6. **Character over credentials, explicitly.** (2024) "I never look at where a candidate has gone to school. Never!" — illustrated with Pete Liegl (sold Forest River on a handshake, set his own $100K salary) and Ben Rosner ("never went past 6th grade"). Buffett's conclusion: "a very large portion of business talent is innate, with nature swamping nurture."

7. **Crisis discipline is pre-loaded, not improvised.** (2008) "When investing, pessimism is your friend, euphoria the enemy." Berkshire deployed $15.6 billion in three weeks during the panic precisely because it always keeps $20B+ in Treasury bills — cash described as "oxygen... never thought about when it is present, the only thing in mind when it is absent."

8. **Distrust of formulas that replace judgment.** "Beware of geeks bearing formulas" (2008, on Black-Scholes misapplied to 100-year options) and "volatility is far from synonymous with risk... popular formulas that equate the two terms lead students, investors and CEOs astray" (2014).

9. **Radical, quantified honesty about failure.** (2024) Buffett notes he used "mistake" or "error" 16 times in letters from 2019-23 alone, versus peer companies that "have never used either word." His warning to his successor Greg Abel: "if you start fooling your shareholders, you will soon believe your own baloney and be fooling yourself as well."

10. **The conglomerate structure is a tax-and-friction-avoidance machine, not just diversification.** (2014) Berkshire can move capital between businesses "without incurring taxes or much in the way of other costs" — an advantage no individual investor or single-industry company has, letting cash from a mature cash-cow (like See's) fund new purchases without the double taxation an independent company would face distributing it.

Sources: the 1977, 1989, 1996, 2008, 2014, and 2024 letters (all from Berkshire Hathaway's own site, berkshirehathaway.com/letters/letters.html).
